# Home & Furniture

## Voice & tone
Warm, considered, and quietly confident. The category sells permanence and care, so copy reads like a thoughtful note from a friend who happens to know about thread count or seasoning a pan — never a salesperson. Avoid hype; favor specificity (the weave, the wood, the weight). Reading level: 7th–9th grade. Sentences are short to medium; rhythm matters more than density.

On-brand openers:
- "Linen gets softer the longer you have it."
- "We made one change to the handle. It changes everything."
- "Three new colors. One we've wanted for years."

Off-brand openers (AI-tells):
- "Discover our amazing collection of premium home essentials!"
- "Transform your living space today with these incredible deals."
- "Elevate your home with our luxurious, must-have items."

Forbidden phrases: "elevate your space," "transform your home," "must-have," "game-changer," "luxurious," "amazing," "unlock," "curated for you" (when not actually curated).

## Visual style markers
- **Color palette:** muted neutrals (oat, bone, clay, sage, charcoal), occasional saturated accent (Caraway perracotta, Floyd's burnt orange). White and off-white dominate backgrounds.
- **Typography:** serif display headlines (Parachute uses a custom serif; Brooklinen leans GT Sectra-adjacent) paired with clean humanist sans (Söhne, Inter, GT America) for body. Generous tracking on small caps for section labels.
- **Imagery:** lifestyle-forward, natural light, lived-in rooms with imperfections (rumpled bed, half-poured coffee). Aspect ratios skew 4:5 portrait for hero, 1:1 for product grids. Flat-lay is reserved for cookware close-ups.
- **Layout density:** low. Lots of whitespace. One idea per scroll-screen.
- **Common motifs:** human hands interacting with the product (folding, pouring, smoothing), morning light, raw materials (wood grain, linen weave, copper).

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: hero image → headline (short) → 1–2 sentence story → single CTA → secondary product row (3 items) → social proof or editorial mention → footer with care/sustainability blurb. Typical section count: 4–6. Discount, when present, lives below the hero — never in the subject line for premium players; it's framed as "the annual sale" or "friends & family," not "% OFF."

## Copy patterns
- **Subject lines:** sensory, declarative, often product-led. 3–6 words. Examples:
  > "The softest sheets we make"
  > "Now in walnut"
  > "💬 Have you seen our sale?" (Brooklinen)
  > "A better way to store leftovers"
  > "Meet the new Dutch oven"
- **Preheader:** completes or contrasts the subject. Examples:
  > "Made from long-staple linen, woven in Portugal."
  > "Limited run. Once it's gone, it's gone."
  > "Comes in five colors you'll actually use."
- **Body length:** 60–150 words. Hero blocks often run 25 words or fewer; supporting blocks 40–80.
- **CTA copy:** verb-led, low-pressure. "Shop the collection," "See the new colors," "Read the story," "Find your size," "Build your set." Avoid "Buy now" — too transactional.
- **Urgency:** light to none. When used, framed as supply ("small batch," "back in stock") not deadline. Heavy countdown timers feel off-brand here.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **Parachute** — sets the bar for moody lifestyle photography and restrained copy.
- **Brooklinen** — slightly more playful voice; strong subject-line economy.
- **Burrow** — modular-furniture story told visually, copy nails the why-we-made-this beat.
- **Floyd** — Detroit-warm tone, honest about materials and durability.
- **Caraway** — color-led, beautifully art-directed product shots; clean typographic system.
- **Made In** — chef-credibility woven in without name-dropping; technical but readable.
- **Year & Day** — editorial calm, gift guides that read like a magazine.
- **Snowe** — tablescape storytelling, sets feel like styled apartments.

## Email types that matter most
New launches (color drops, collaborations), seasonal refreshes (linen swaps, candle scents), restocks, editorial/recipe content (for cookware), care-and-keep guides (sustainability/longevity story), bundle and registry pushes, and the annual sale (Memorial Day, Black Friday, Friends & Family).

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. Generic "elevate/transform your space" framing instead of a specific sensory detail.
2. Stacking adjectives ("luxurious, premium, beautifully crafted") rather than naming one material trait.
3. Lifestyle photography described in copy ("imagine yourself relaxing…") instead of letting the image do the work.
4. CTA labeled "Shop Now" universally — real brands vary the verb to match the offer.
5. Subject lines that telegraph promo ("Save 20% on our bestsellers!") — premium home brands almost never lead with discount in the subject.
