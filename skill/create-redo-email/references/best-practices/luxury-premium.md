# Luxury & Premium (DTC)

## Voice & tone
Quiet, considered, and confident enough to under-explain. Luxury copy says less and trusts the reader to lean in. There is no exclamation, no exhortation, and almost no second-person sales address. The voice is closer to editorial or a museum wall label than to a marketing email. Reading level: 9th-11th grade — vocabulary skews older, but sentences stay tight. Tense is often present-continuous and observational ("a balm formulated for…") rather than imperative.

On-brand openers:
- "A formulation revisited."
- "Three years in development. Now available."
- "Notes of cedar, oakmoss, and the late evening."

Off-brand openers (AI tells):
- "Indulge in the pinnacle of luxury with our exclusive new collection!"
- "Experience unparalleled elegance and sophistication today."
- "Elevate your lifestyle with these premium, must-have essentials."

Forbidden phrases: "indulge," "pinnacle of luxury," "unparalleled," "exclusive offer," "must-have," "elevate your lifestyle," "limited time only" (and any all-caps urgency), "treat yourself," "premium quality," "sophistication," "for the discerning customer."

## Visual style markers
- Color palette: warm neutrals — bone, cream, oat, taupe, amber, charcoal. Black-on-cream and amber-on-cream are signature. Pure white is rare; warm off-white is the default canvas. Brand color is a near-monochrome statement (Aēsop's amber, Diptyque's oval, Hermès orange used as accent only).
- Typography: serifs lead, almost always. Didot, Garamond, Caslon, custom serifs (Aēsop uses Suisse Works; Diptyque sets its own). Italics for product names. Sans is reserved for body copy and is small, generously leaded. Letter-spacing is wide on display; tracking is a stylistic choice.
- Imagery: still life, single-product close-up on a textured surface (marble, linen, raw wood), or atmospheric in-context (the Diptyque candle on a side table at dusk). No models, or single model in editorial repose. Aspect ratios skew square or 4:5. Photography is the design — minimal overlay.
- Layout density: very low. One image, one paragraph, one CTA per send is common. Whitespace is the brand.
- Common motifs: object-on-surface still life, single editorial portrait, ingredient or material plate (a sprig of bergamot, a swatch of leather), founder or maker portrait, archival illustration (Diptyque, Hermès).

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: single editorial image (often full-bleed, often the entire above-fold) → one short paragraph of considered copy → one CTA → optional small footer story or "also new" with one or two pieces.

Typical section count: 2-4 blocks. Half what beauty or apparel runs.

Discount placement: typically absent. Aēsop runs no discounts publicly. Diptyque limits promotional language to "complimentary engraving" or "complimentary shipping above X." When premium brands run actual percent-off, they call it "private sale" or "members' preview" and frame it as access, not price. The percentage is rarely the headline.

## Copy patterns
Subject lines: short, noun-led, declarative. Often a product name alone or a single-line poetic fragment. 2-6 words. No emoji. Sentence case or title case; never all-caps.

- > "Cleanse, tone, hone." (Aēsop)
- > "A new chapter for Philosykos." (Diptyque-style)
- > "The Cuyana System." (Cuyana)
- > "Made for the desk and the dinner." (Buck Mason-style)
- > "Introducing the Saddle Bag." (Loewe / luxury fashion pattern)

Preheader: extends the subject with a material, place, or detail. Never repeats the subject. Often functions as the second line of a couplet.

- > "A revisited balm, now in 50ml."
- > "Crafted in our Brooklyn workshop."
- > "Notes of fig leaf, green almond, white cedar."

Body copy: 40-100 words is the sweet spot. Aēsop occasionally goes longer in welcome and brand-story sends (literary cadence). Sentences read like museum cards or perfume liner notes — observational, specific, light on adjectives, heavy on materials and process. Avoid superlatives. Name the maker, the place, the ingredient, the technique.

CTAs: minimal, often a single phrase. Sometimes just the product name as a link.

- > "Discover the collection"
- > "Read more"
- > "Explore"
- > "Visit a store"
- > "Request a sample"

Urgency/scarcity: rarely employed. Scarcity, if it appears, is structural ("limited edition of 200," "available in select boutiques") rather than promotional. No countdowns. No "ends tonight." The implied scarcity is access — "by invitation," "members only," "available in selected cities" — not a clock.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **Aēsop** — literary copy, amber-on-cream palette, no discounts ever, considered cadence (low frequency, high signal), in-store rituals echoed in email design.
- **Diptyque** — Parisian heritage voice, oval-medallion motif, fragrance-note storytelling, illustrated headers in seasonal sends.
- **Hermès** — orange-as-restraint, archival illustrations, almost zero direct selling in email, equestrian and craft references.
- **Loewe** — art-world adjacency, Jonathan Anderson-era cultural collaborations highlighted in editorial sends.
- **Buck Mason** — premium Americana, denim and milled goods, longer-form storytelling in welcome flows, restrained promo cadence.
- **Cuyana** — "fewer, better" mantra, monogram-as-feature, sand-and-cream palette, leather-care storytelling.
- **Le Labo** — personalization (the city, the date, the recipient name on the label) as the entire brand, deliberately rough typography, low send frequency.
- **Khaite** — fashion-house editorial style, single editorial image as the email, minimal copy, runway-led campaigns.

## Email types that matter most
- **Collection or formulation launches** — the central send. Treated as editorial event, not promotion. Often a single image with one paragraph and one CTA.
- **Brand-story / founder / craft sends** — periodic deep dives on a maker, a material, a place. Build the desire reservoir that premium positioning runs on.
- **Welcome series (2-4 emails)** — house values, formulation philosophy, store invitation (for brands with retail), care instructions. Discount is absent or framed as a sample/complimentary item.
- **Private sale / members' preview** — the only acceptable promotional construct. Framed around access, not savings.
- **In-store / event invitations** — fragrance discovery sessions, trunk shows, openings. Drives prestige and IRL engagement.
- **Replenishment and care** — calm, infrequent prompts (Aēsop's hand wash replenishment is the archetype). Tone is reminder, not pitch.
- **Seasonal editorials** — holiday gifting framed as gestures, not gifts; rendered with the same restraint as a standard send.

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. The word "indulge" anywhere — luxury copy never asks the reader to indulge; it presents and trusts.
2. Adjective inflation ("unparalleled," "exquisite," "pinnacle," "ultimate") where a real luxury brand cites a single concrete detail (the supplier, the year, the technique).
3. Discount language or urgency cues — premium brands either avoid promo entirely or frame it as access; "Limited time! 20% off!" reads instantly downmarket.
4. Stacked CTAs (3+ buttons) — luxury sends carry one CTA, sometimes embedded as a text link.
5. Generic lifestyle photography (smiling models, white-toothed groups) instead of object-on-surface still life or a single editorial portrait — real luxury imagery has air around the subject.
