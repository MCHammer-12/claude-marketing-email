# Supplements & Wellness

## Voice & tone
Calm, credible, and quietly authoritative. Wellness DTC writes like a knowledgeable friend who happens to be a clinician — not a hype salesperson. Reading level is 7th-9th grade. Sentences are clear, often declarative. Trust is the product; tone reflects that.

On-brand openers:
- "Here's what 90 days looks like."
- "Most multivitamins miss the basics."
- "A small change. Compounded daily."

Off-brand openers (AI tells):
- "Unlock your wellness journey..."
- "Transform your health with our revolutionary..."
- "Discover the power of nature with..."

Forbidden phrases: "wellness journey," "unlock your potential," "boost your immunity," "supercharge your," "the secret to," "game-changing," "feel your best self," "holistic approach."

## Visual style markers
- Color palette: Muted earth tones — sage, ivory, terracotta, soft black. Ritual leans on pastel mint and warm cream. AG1 is monochrome black/white with a single accent. Hims uses bold sage green and editorial neutrals. Avoid medical-blue; that reads pharma, not wellness.
- Typography: Editorial serifs for headlines (Tiempos, Söhne, GT Sectra) paired with clean humanist sans for body (Inter, GT America). Weights stay moderate (400-600). Spacing is generous.
- Imagery: Mix of ingredient close-ups (turmeric root, raw greens, capsules in hand), aspirational lifestyle (morning routines, sunlight), and editorial product shots on neutral backgrounds. Founders and real customers appear in win-back and trust emails. 1:1 and 4:5 primary, 16:9 for hero stories.
- Layout density: Medium-low. Whitespace signals quality. Long-scroll story emails are common (AG1's "Day 1 to Day 90" series).
- Common motifs: Checkmark comparison tables ("Us vs. them"), ingredient breakdowns, founder photos, science badges (third-party tested, NSF certified), morning/evening usage rituals.

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: editorial hero (lifestyle or ingredient shot) → headline that names a problem or benefit → 2-3 short paragraphs of evidence/story → social proof (review, press, doctor quote) → comparison or ingredient breakdown → CTA → footer with subscription perks.

Typical section count: 4-6. Story emails run longer than other segments because trust-building takes real estate.

Discount placement: Welcome offer (often 20-25% first order or first subscription month) is usually buried below the brand story — never the headline. New-customer urgency is soft. Subscription emails rarely discount at all; they sell consistency.

## Copy patterns
Subject lines lean on benefit, curiosity, or question framing. Examples:
> "Where'd you go?" (Magic Spoon-style, used widely in wellness win-backs)
> "Your 90-day check-in" (AG1)
> "What's actually in your multivitamin?" (Ritual-style)
> "A small upgrade for your routine" (AG1)
> "We made this for you" (Care/of personalization angle)

Preheader: Used to clarify and disarm. Examples:
> "No fillers. No fluff. Just what your body needs."
> "Backed by clinical research."
> "Plus, 20% off your first month."

Body copy length: 100-250 words is typical. Story-led emails (AG1, Seed) push 400-600. Tone stays measured — wellness customers distrust hype.

CTA verbs are clear and commitment-light:
> "Build your routine"
> "Start your subscription"
> "Get matched"
> "Learn more"
> "Take the quiz"

Urgency: Light to never. Wellness brands sell repeatable habits, not impulse buys. Countdowns and "ENDS TONIGHT" feel off-brand. Restock and "your next shipment" timing creates natural urgency without manufactured scarcity.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **AG1** — masterclass in objection-handling welcome series, "day-by-day" results framing.
- **Ritual** — transparent ingredient sourcing, editorial design, founder voice.
- **Seed** — long-form scientific storytelling, premium typography, restrained color.
- **Hims & Hers** — destigmatizes prescription wellness, conversational but credible.
- **Care/of** — personalization-led ("we made this for you"), warm tone.
- **Athletic Greens** (now AG1) — same brand, worth noting separately for pre-rebrand inspo.
- **Olipop** — straddles food/bev and wellness; useful for gut-health framing.
- **Function Health** — clinical authority, data-as-content emails.

## Email types that matter most
- **Welcome series (5-8 emails)** — the trust-builder. Each email handles one objection: "What is this?" "Why is it different?" "Is it safe?" "Is it worth the price?" "How do I use it?"
- **Subscription onboarding** — first 30 days post-signup, sets habit expectations.
- **Day/Week/Month check-ins** — "How are you feeling at day 14?" reinforces the routine.
- **Ingredient deep-dives** — single-ingredient education emails build credibility.
- **Win-back** — "Your routine misses you" framing, never aggressive discount.
- **Quiz/personalization** — drives the most engagement; positions brand as advisor.
- **Replenishment** — auto-ship reminders styled as care, not transaction.

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. Vague benefit claims ("supports overall wellness," "promotes a healthy lifestyle") without specifics.
2. Listing every ingredient as "premium" or "clinically-studied" without sourcing.
3. Overuse of "your journey" — real brands talk about routines, habits, results.
4. Stock imagery of yoga poses, smoothie bowls, or generic green-juice flat-lays.
5. Asking the reader to "embrace" or "discover" something — wellness brands instruct, not invite.
