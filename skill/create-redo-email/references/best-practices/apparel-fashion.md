# Apparel & Fashion (DTC)

## Voice & tone
Confident, plainspoken, and a little dry. The shopper is already in on the joke — emails read like a friend texting about a new drop, not a brand pitching. Avoid hype; let the garment and the photo carry weight. Reading level: 7th grade. Sentences are short, often fragments. Pronouns matter — "you" and "we," rarely "our customers."

On-brand openers:
- "New shape. Same fit."
- "Three colors. One is selling out."
- "It's back. (For real this time.)"

Off-brand openers (AI tells):
- "We are thrilled to announce the launch of our exciting new collection!"
- "Discover the perfect addition to your wardrobe with our latest arrivals."
- "Elevate your style with these must-have pieces designed for the modern individual."

Forbidden phrases: "elevate," "curated for you," "must-have," "thrilled to announce," "luxurious feel," "perfect for any occasion," "wardrobe staple" (overused), "discover."

## Visual style markers
- Color palette: muted neutrals (bone, oat, charcoal, washed denim), occasional brand accent. Backgrounds are usually off-white or product-colored, not pure white.
- Typography: serif display (think Tiempos, Canela, GT Sectra) paired with a clean grotesque sans (Aktiv Grotesk, Söhne, Inter) for body. Weights stay light-to-regular; bold is rare and load-bearing when used.
- Imagery: editorial lifestyle on real bodies, shot in natural light. Aspect ratios skew tall (4:5, 3:4) for the hero, square or 16:9 below. Flat-lays appear for product detail callouts, not heroes.
- Layout density: low. One product, one idea per block. Generous whitespace. Allbirds and Everlane often ship emails with 3-4 image blocks total.
- Common motifs: full-bleed hero, single-product close-up, "as worn by" UGC strip, fabric/material detail shot.

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: hero image (full-bleed, with overlay or below-image headline) → one-sentence story → product grid (2-up or 3-up) → social proof or material story → secondary CTA → footer with size guide, returns, and gift-card link.

Typical section count: 4-6 blocks.

Discount placement: when present, it lives in the hero or as a thin top banner. Brands like Everlane and Bombas often skip discount language entirely in favor of "new" or "back in stock." When a promo runs (Allbirds, Aritzia sales), the percentage is the headline.

## Copy patterns
Subject lines: short, declarative, occasionally cryptic. 2-5 words is normal. Lowercase is common for casual brands; sentence case for the more polished.

- > "Your Butt Has Summer Plans" (Everlane)
- > "Change is a step away" (Everlane)
- > "back. better." (Aritzia restock pattern)
- > "Now in: the wool runner" (Allbirds-style)
- > "A little something for your feet." (Bombas)

Preheader: completes or undercuts the subject. Used to seed product name, price drop, or "ends tonight."

- > "The denim short, restocked in three new washes."
- > "Free shipping on orders over $75. Today only."
- > "Plus: a peek at what's next."

Body copy: 30-80 words total per email is normal for the casual end (Allbirds, Bombas). Editorial brands (Aritzia, Buck Mason) push to 120-180. Sentences are short. Paragraphs rarely exceed two sentences.

CTAs: verb-led, two or three words.
- > "Shop the drop"
- > "See the fit"
- > "Get yours"
- > "Find your size"
- > "Read the story"

Urgency/scarcity: light. "Selling fast," "back in limited quantities," "ends Sunday." Never countdown timers, never all-caps "HURRY." Restock and waitlist mechanics replace artificial scarcity.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **Everlane** — radical transparency in pricing copy, sparse design, subject lines that bait without lying.
- **Aritzia** — editorial photography, serif-driven layouts, treats every drop like a magazine cover.
- **Allbirds** — sustainability woven into product copy (carbon footprint per shoe), minimalist art direction.
- **Bombas** — warm, one-for-one mission front and center, friendly tone, strong subject-line wit.
- **Buck Mason** — Americana voice, denim-heavy palette, long-form storytelling in welcome flows.
- **Cuyana** — "fewer, better" mantra in every send, restrained palette, monogram-as-feature.
- **Outerknown** — surf-culture voice, ocean palette, founder (Kelly Slater) presence in copy.
- **Vuori** — California-active voice, lifestyle UGC, lighter promotional tone than competitors.

## Email types that matter most
- **Welcome series (3-5 emails)** — brand story, fit/sizing primer, first-purchase incentive. Apparel brands lean heavily on the fit-confidence email because returns are the category killer.
- **New drops / restocks** — the workhorse send. Often weekly. Restock notifications are a top revenue driver.
- **Abandoned cart** — image-first, single-product focus, free-shipping or returns reassurance in the body. Discount only in the second send.
- **Post-purchase styling** — "how to wear it" content 7-14 days after delivery, paired with a review request.
- **Seasonal transitions** — spring/fall edits are the marquee campaigns; subject lines reference weather or activity, not the season name directly.
- **Sale events** — fewer than competitors; when they run, they run hard with daily reminders for 3-5 days.

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. Overuse of "elevate," "curated," and "wardrobe essentials" — apparel brands say "pieces," "fit," or just name the garment.
2. Adjective stacking ("luxuriously soft, beautifully tailored, effortlessly versatile") where a real brand picks one detail and earns it.
3. Subject lines that summarize the email instead of teasing it ("Check out our new spring collection!").
4. Generic lifestyle copy with no specific garment, fabric, or fit reference — real apparel emails always anchor to a product.
5. CTAs like "Click here to learn more" or "Browse our collection" instead of "Shop the tee" or "See the fit."
