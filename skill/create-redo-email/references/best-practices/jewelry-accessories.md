# Jewelry & Accessories (DTC)

## Voice & tone
Considered, a little romantic, but never overwrought. Jewelry sits between fashion and gift — copy honors both the buyer-for-self and the buyer-for-someone-else without picking a side awkwardly. Tone leans intimate and quiet at the modern-fine end (Mejuri, Catbird, Cuyana) and warmer / more playful at the demi-fine end (BaubleBar, Ana Luisa). Reading level: 7th-9th grade. First person plural is rare; second person ("yours," "your everyday") does most of the work.

On-brand openers:
- "Made to be layered. Made to be lived in."
- "The hoop you'll reach for tomorrow, and the day after."
- "Solid gold. Half the price you'd expect."

Off-brand openers (AI tells):
- "Adorn yourself with timeless elegance and exquisite craftsmanship."
- "Discover the perfect piece to celebrate every precious moment."
- "Elevate your jewelry collection with our stunning new arrivals."

Forbidden phrases: "adorn," "exquisite," "timeless elegance" (overdone), "stunning," "treasured piece," "sparkle and shine," "every precious moment," "the perfect gift for her," "make a statement."

## Visual style markers
- Color palette: warm neutrals — cream, sand, bone, soft taupe. Black accents at the modern end (Catbird, Mejuri). Brand metallics (gold, rose gold, silver) treated as a near-second palette. Pastels appear at the demi-fine and gift-y end (Kendra Scott).
- Typography: high-contrast serifs as the hero (Bodoni-adjacent, Didot, GT Super), often italic. Body in a quiet sans (Söhne, Maison Neue) or a small-scale serif. Tracking is wide on display lines.
- Imagery: macro product on neutral surfaces (linen, marble, skin), close-cropped hand/ear/neck shots that show scale, and "stacked" or "layered" hero shots that demonstrate the styling thesis. Aspect ratios: square for product, tall (4:5) for body/hand crops. Pure flat-lays appear but rarely without a hand for scale.
- Layout density: low to medium. Lots of room. Single product per block; jewelry doesn't grid well at small sizes.
- Common motifs: hand-with-rings close-up, "stack" or "layer" arrangement on linen, dust-bag / packaging shot, metal-and-material callout (14k solid gold, freshwater pearl), behind-the-bench artisan photo.

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: editorial hero (single piece or styled stack) → one-line story or styling cue → product grid (often 2-up, never more than 3-up) → materials / craftsmanship block → gift-or-styling secondary block → CTA → footer with sizing guide (ring, chain length), engraving/personalization link, and lifetime-warranty or "made to last" reassurance.

Typical section count: 4-6 blocks.

Discount placement: muted at the modern-fine end — Mejuri, Cuyana, and Catbird mostly avoid percent-off and lean on "new drop," "back in stock," or "last few." When discounts exist (friends-and-family, end-of-season), they sit in a thin top banner or get a dedicated send. Demi-fine brands (BaubleBar, Kendra Scott) use percent-off more openly.

## Copy patterns
Subject lines: short and noun-led. Often a single image or styling cue. 3-7 words.

- > "Just a few pieces left..." (Catbird)
- > "I want it. I got it." (BaubleBar)
- > "Mix & Layer Your Amulets" (David Yurman)
- > "How to style our new pieces" (Monica Vinader)
- > "Play it cool in Silver" (Monica Vinader)

Preheader: completes the thought with material, scale, or scarcity.

- > "Many have sold out — these are still here."
- > "14k solid gold. Available through Sunday."
- > "Now in three new lengths."

Body copy: 60-120 words for most sends. Materials and craft language earn their space — "14k solid gold," "hand-set," "made in our Brooklyn studio." Don't pile adjectives; pick a detail. Story-driven sends (founder pieces, collection launches) can stretch to 200+.

CTAs: short, often quietly possessive.
- > "Shop the stack"
- > "See the collection"
- > "Make it yours"
- > "Find your size"
- > "Get on the list"

Urgency/scarcity: a defining lever. Limited runs, "only a few left," waitlists, and made-to-order timelines do most of the work that countdown timers do elsewhere. Catbird's "Just a few pieces left..." is the archetype. Avoid loud "HURRY!" — let the scarcity be matter-of-fact.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **Mejuri** — "fine jewelry for everyday," weekday-drop cadence, education-led sends (e.g., "what is vermeil?"), warm minimalist art direction.
- **Catbird** — Brooklyn-studio voice, scarcity-driven subject lines, social proof via press pull-quotes (NYT, Vogue), tiny-text romanticism.
- **Ana Luisa** — accessible-trend positioning, values-led welcome flow (sustainability, transparency), double opt-in.
- **Cuyana** — "fewer, better" mantra extends to leather and small accessories, monogram-as-feature, restrained palette.
- **BaubleBar** — playful demi-fine voice, color-heavy palette, personalization (initial pieces) front and center.
- **Monica Vinader** — styling-cue subject lines ("Mix & Layer"), recycled silver/gold sustainability story, clean sans on cream.
- **Kendra Scott** — color-and-stone storytelling, gift-occasion campaigns, lighter promo tone.
- **Aurate** — solid-gold transparency story (price breakdown like Everlane's), modern editorial photography.

## Email types that matter most
- **New collection launches** — the marquee send. Often weekly or biweekly. Treated as editorial moments, not promos.
- **Restock / waitlist hit** — high conversion. Triggered, single-product, one CTA.
- **Welcome series (3-4 emails)** — brand story, materials primer (gold karats, gemstone care), sizing guide (ring measure tools, chain lengths). The sizing email is non-negotiable for return prevention.
- **Gifting campaigns** — heavy Q4, Mother's Day, Valentine's, anniversary. Often include guided "for her / for him / under $100" filters and gift-wrap callouts.
- **Personalization / engraving promos** — drive higher AOV; usually framed around occasions.
- **Abandoned cart** — single-piece hero, scale shot (on body), free returns and lifetime-warranty reassurance. Discount almost never in send one for modern-fine brands.
- **Care and "live in your pieces" educational** — extends lifetime value; "how to clean your gold," "why your chain knots and what to do."

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. "Adorn yourself" / "exquisite craftsmanship" / "timeless elegance" — real jewelry brands name materials and scale, not feelings about jewelry in general.
2. Generic "perfect gift" framing without a specific occasion, recipient, or piece — real brands say "for her 30th" or "the friend who never wears yellow gold."
3. Sparkle / shine / luminous adjective clusters where a real brand cites karat, finish, or stone cut.
4. Lifestyle photography that doesn't show scale — jewelry emails almost always include a hand, ear, or neck for proportion.
5. Treating every send as a sale event — modern-fine brands lean on drops and restocks; constant percent-off framing reads downmarket or AI-formulaic.
