# Outdoor & Sporting

## Voice & tone
Earnest, capable, and slightly understated. This category sells participation more than product — the email is often a small piece of a story about a run, a route, a season, or a fight worth picking. Voice is closer to a coach or trail buddy than a copywriter. Lean on the verbs of the sport (climb, send, push, log, lace up). Earned authority beats hype. Reading level: 7th–10th grade. Sentences run shorter when describing effort, longer when telling a story.

On-brand openers:
- "Five miles before sunrise. That's the deal."
- "We rebuilt the shell. Same jacket, less weight."
- "The river opens Saturday. Here's what to pack."

Off-brand openers (AI-tells):
- "Gear up for your next adventure with our amazing collection!"
- "Discover the ultimate performance apparel for athletes!"
- "Unleash your inner athlete with our premium products!"

Forbidden phrases: "unleash," "inner athlete," "gear up" (cliché), "level up," "next adventure," "ultimate performance," "conquer," "crush your goals," "epic." Also avoid generic eco-virtue ("sustainable," "eco-friendly") without specifics — Patagonia and Cotopaxi name a material, a factory, or a percentage.

## Visual style markers
- **Color palette:** earth and field — clay, moss, slate, ochre, navy. Patagonia/REI accent with high-vis safety colors (Tracksmith's signature red, Cotopaxi's color-blocked brights). Black-and-white treatment shows up in heritage/race-day emails.
- **Typography:** athletic-grotesque sans (Tracksmith's GT America-feeling display, Söhne, Founders Grotesk) often paired with a serif for editorial passages. Headlines are large, sometimes uppercase, with tight tracking. Numbers (mileage, splits, vert) are visual hero elements.
- **Imagery:** action photography in real conditions (rain, mud, dusk), often grainy or filmic. Athletes are mid-effort, not posed. Aspect ratios skew 16:9 cinematic for hero, 4:5 for product. Landscape wide-shots open most campaigns. Models look like the customer, not the customer's fantasy.
- **Layout density:** medium. More text than home-furniture because the story matters. Editorial multi-section emails are common (newsletter-style B-Mail, Tracksmith's Meter).
- **Common motifs:** topographic lines, race bibs, weather data, route maps, hand-drawn marks, scanned-paper textures (Tracksmith), gradient-blocked color fields (Cotopaxi).

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: cinematic hero image → editorial headline → 1–2 paragraph story (athlete, place, design decision) → CTA into longer-read or product → product row tied to the story → "what we're reading/listening to/racing" footer block. Typical section count: 5–7 (heavier than home goods). Discount lives near the bottom or in a dedicated section labeled "Pro Deals" / "Member Pricing" — premium players (Tracksmith, Patagonia, Bandit) rarely promo at all outside specific moments.

## Copy patterns
- **Subject lines:** declarative, often sentence-length, frequently borrow from sports vernacular. 4–10 words. Examples:
  > "Running is a Gift" (Tracksmith)
  > "We Know You Hate To Lose. Anything." (Tracksmith)
  > "Don't Buy This Jacket" (Patagonia, iconic)
  > "New from the Mill" (Tracksmith)
  > "The B-Mail: NYC running, fortnightly" (Bandit Running)
- **Preheader:** extends the story, often credits an athlete or place. Examples:
  > "Notes from the team in Flagstaff."
  > "Built for the wet shoulder seasons."
  > "How members get more out of the co-op."
- **Body length:** 120–300 words is common; editorial issues run 400+. Less compressed than other categories because readers expect a read.
- **CTA copy:** action-led, sport-specific. "Read the dispatch," "See the kit," "Find a trip," "Become a member," "Shop the team kit," "Get the route." Avoid "Shop now."
- **Urgency:** mostly absent. When used, framed as practical (seasonal window, race-day delivery cutoff) rather than manufactured. Cotopaxi runs occasional flash drops; Patagonia avoids urgency almost entirely.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **Patagonia** — story-first, activism-integrated; product is often a footnote to the cause.
- **REI** — co-op voice, member-centric, expert/educational tone.
- **Tracksmith** — best-in-class editorial design, runner's-magazine feel, restraint with promo.
- **Bandit Running** — NYC-rooted, community-as-content, smart-aleck-friendly but disciplined.
- **Outerknown** — surf heritage, Kelly Slater pedigree, sustainability with specifics.
- **Cotopaxi** — color-led product story, B-corp purpose woven into the buy.
- **On Running** — Swiss-engineered precision, science and design language, athlete-led narrative.
- **Topo Designs** — Colorado outdoor-utility voice, retro color story, gear-as-system.

## Email types that matter most
Editorial/dispatch newsletters (the highest-craft work in this category), race recaps, athlete features, product drops tied to seasons or trips, member/loyalty announcements (REI Co-op, Tracksmith Fellowship), trip and event invites, gear guides (kit-building), activism and impact updates (1% for the Planet, voting, public lands).

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. Generic "adventure" language with no specific place, route, or distance named.
2. Stock action-photo descriptions ("conquer the mountain") instead of pointing at a real trail/race/season.
3. Sustainability claims without numbers or supplier names — real brands say "Bluesign-approved" or "65% recycled polyester from Taiwan," not "eco-friendly fabric."
4. CTA stuck on "Shop Now" — actual brands use sport-verbs ("Lace up," "Log it," "Read the dispatch").
5. Subject lines that pile on adjectives ("Premium performance gear for athletes!") — the real ones are a single sentence, often a fragment, that sounds like something a coach would say.
