# Pet Supplies

## Voice & tone
Warm, playful, and unapologetically silly — pet emails are emotional and treat the pet as the customer, not the owner. Reading level is 5th-7th grade. Puns are encouraged. The brand talks like a friend who is also obsessed with your dog. Health/Rx-adjacent brands (The Farmer's Dog, vet) dial back the goofiness slightly but keep warmth.

On-brand openers:
- "Bella's looking at you."
- "Heads up, dog parents."
- "We packed something special for Max this month."

Off-brand openers (AI tells):
- "Treat your beloved companion to..."
- "As a pet owner, you understand..."
- "Discover premium products for your furry friend."

Forbidden phrases: "furry friend," "four-legged family member," "fur baby" (overused; use sparingly and only when on-brand), "pawsome" (cliché), "tail-wagging deals," "treat your pet to," "premium pet care."

## Visual style markers
- Color palette: Bright and saturated for play-focused brands (BarkBox uses bold pinks, oranges, kraft brown). Earthy and trustworthy for food/vet brands (The Farmer's Dog uses warm beige, sage, deep navy). Chewy uses primary blue + white for a friendly retail feel.
- Typography: Rounded sans-serifs (DM Sans, Nunito, Poppins) for play-focused brands. Editorial serifs (Tiempos, Söhne) for food/health-positioned brands. Handwritten or display fonts often used for accent moments and pet "signatures."
- Imagery: Real dogs, real cats — high volume of UGC and customer pet photos. Hero shots are pet-first (the pet enjoying the product, not the product alone). Illustrated mascots and characters are common (BarkBox themes each box like an episode). 1:1 dominates because pet photos are usually square.
- Layout density: Medium-high for play brands (lots of products, themes, characters in one email); medium-low for food/Rx brands (clean, trust-forward).
- Common visual motifs: Pet name personalization in headers, illustrated paw prints/bones used sparingly, themed monthly boxes with character art, "before/after" food transition photos, packaging callouts.

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: pet-first hero image (often a customer dog) → personalized greeting using pet's name → short brand voice setup → product/box reveal or content block → social proof (often UGC pet photos) → CTA → footer with subscription details.

Typical section count: 4-7. BarkBox-style monthly box reveals run longer because they're unpacking the theme. Vet/food brands stay tighter.

Discount placement: Subscription brands lead with the offer ("Get 60% off your first box" — The Farmer's Dog). Play brands hide promos inside the theme reveal. Welcome emails always include the offer in the headline or hero.

## Copy patterns
Subject lines lean on pet-name personalization, curiosity, and warmth. Examples:
> "Bella, your June box just shipped" (BarkBox-style personalization)
> "A fresh start for [Pet Name]" (The Farmer's Dog)
> "Heads up, dog parents" (BarkBox)
> "Your dog deserves better than this" (The Farmer's Dog, problem-aware)
> "Max's autoship is on the way" (Chewy)

Preheader: Reinforces personalization or adds soft urgency. Examples:
> "Plus, a peek at next month's theme."
> "60% off your first box ends Sunday."
> "We picked toys based on Bella's chew style."

Body copy length: 80-180 words for play brands. 150-300 for food/health brands (more education and transition guidance).

CTA verbs are warm and committal:
> "Build your box"
> "Get started"
> "Customize your plan"
> "Shop the theme"
> "Reorder Max's food"

Urgency: Moderate. Subscription brands use natural shipping cadence and "your dog's plan starts soon" framing. Avoid harsh countdowns — pet customers respond to care, not pressure.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **BarkBox / BARK** — gold standard for playful voice, themed monthly content, dog-as-character storytelling.
- **The Farmer's Dog** — premium fresh food, dog-name personalization, trust-led copy.
- **Chewy** — retail volume done warmly, world-class customer service emails (including the famous condolence cards).
- **Ollie** — fresh food competitor to Farmer's Dog, lifestyle photography, transparent ingredient sourcing.
- **Petco / Petsmart** — large catalog, pet-name personalization at scale.
- **Wild Earth** — plant-based dog food, science-forward messaging.
- **Spot & Tango** — fresh dog food with strong before/after testimonial use.
- **Cat Person** — rare cat-first brand with editorial design and feline-specific voice.

## Email types that matter most
- **Welcome series with pet profile capture** — first email usually asks dog/cat/age/breed to personalize everything downstream.
- **Monthly box reveal (subscription play brands)** — themed announcement is the hero campaign of the month.
- **Replenishment/autoship reminders** — food and treats run on consistent reorder cadence; this is the lifeblood email.
- **Transition guides (food brands)** — multi-email series helping owners switch food gradually.
- **Birthday/Gotcha Day** — pet's "birthday" is a real revenue moment; emails get sentimental and promotional.
- **Post-purchase content** — "How to introduce Max to his new toy" video emails extend engagement.
- **Win-back** — "We miss Bella" framing works because the dog is the customer.

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. Overuse of "furry friend" or "fur baby" in every paragraph — real brands use the pet's actual name.
2. Generic pet-parent flattery ("you're the best pet parent") without specific context.
3. Stock images of golden retrievers in flower fields — real brands use customer dogs of all breeds and sizes.
4. Treating dog and cat owners interchangeably — real brands segment hard.
5. Cliché puns ("pawsome," "purr-fect," "fur-ever") used as the only humor lever instead of actual voice.
