# Beauty & Skincare (DTC)

## Voice & tone
Intimate and conversational with a wink. Beauty emails read like a group chat with someone who actually knows the ingredients. The line moves between two poles: clinical-but-warm (Drunk Elephant, Nécessaire, The Ordinary) and playful-bestie (Fenty, Glossier, Ouai). Both stay first-person plural and avoid corporate distance. Reading level: 6th-8th grade. Lowercase styling is common in subject lines for the playful end.

On-brand openers:
- "Meet the serum that's been on our nightstand for a month."
- "Three drops. That's it. That's the routine."
- "We tested it. Then we tested it on our moms."

Off-brand openers (AI tells):
- "Unlock your skin's true potential with our revolutionary new formula!"
- "Indulge in a luxurious self-care experience with our premium skincare line."
- "Discover the secret to radiant, glowing skin with these must-have products."

Forbidden phrases: "unlock," "radiance" (overdone), "self-care" as marketing copy, "indulge," "luxurious experience," "transform your skin," "youthful glow," "boost your confidence," "your best skin ever," "revolutionary formula."

## Visual style markers
- Color palette: brand-determined and load-bearing. Glossier owns millennial pink; Drunk Elephant owns primary brights on white; Fenty owns black-and-magenta; Nécessaire owns putty/sage neutrals; Aēsop owns warm beige and forest. Backgrounds frequently match product packaging color.
- Typography: a single hero face does most of the work — Apercu (Glossier), bold display serifs (Fenty, Charlotte Tilbury), or stark grotesques (The Ordinary). Body copy is small, generously leaded.
- Imagery: macro product shots, swatch close-ups on real skin tones, hand-holding-product hero, occasional founder portrait. Aspect ratios mix square hero with tall product columns. Real skin (pores visible, no over-retouching) is a tell of the better brands.
- Layout density: medium. More product callouts than apparel, because shoppers want ingredient and shade info before clicking.
- Common motifs: ingredient callout boxes ("hyaluronic acid 2%"), before/after split (used sparingly by the credible brands), routine-builder grids (morning/night), shade swatches as a horizontal strip.

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: product hero with macro detail → one-line value prop → key ingredient or claim block (3-up icons or callouts) → how-to-use or routine slot → social proof (review pull-quote or UGC) → CTA → footer with shade finder, ingredient glossary, sample link.

Typical section count: 5-7 blocks. Beauty emails carry more density than apparel because the buyer needs to feel informed before adding to cart.

Discount placement: usually a thin top banner or a footer line ("free shipping at $40"). The product, not the price, leads. Friends-and-family events are the exception — those put the percentage in the hero. Sample-with-purchase is more common than percent-off as a tactic.

## Copy patterns
Subject lines: short, conversational, often emoji-punctuated for the playful brands. 4-8 words. Curiosity gaps are common. Glossier averages ~22 characters.

- > "You forgot something" (Fenty Beauty)
- > "Still available: You. Need. This." (Fenty Beauty)
- > "Gloss Bomb Dip got NEW SHADES!" (Fenty Beauty)
- > "New Glossier serums are here!" (Glossier)
- > "Sooo good for smoochin'" (Fenty Beauty)

Preheader: ingredient name, shade count, or a second beat of personality.

- > "Niacinamide 10%, zinc 1%. The one your derm keeps recommending."
- > "Now in 12 new shades, including 4 deep neutrals."
- > "Plus a free deluxe sample with every order this week."

Body copy: 80-150 words for product launches. Routine-builder and educational sends can stretch to 200-250. Sentences are short. Ingredient names get bolded or pulled into chips. The credibility brands (Drunk Elephant, Nécessaire) front-load the actives by name and percentage.

CTAs: verb-led, sometimes playful.
- > "Add to routine"
- > "Find my shade"
- > "Shop the set"
- > "Try it now"
- > "Restock my favorite"

Urgency/scarcity: light to moderate. Limited shade drops and "back in stock" notifications drive more urgency than countdown timers. Sale events ramp from "early access" to "ends tonight" over 3-5 days. Fenty leans more aggressive ("Cyber Week Early Access: 30% OFF SITEWIDE") than the clinical brands.

## Distinguished reference brands
- **Glossier** — pink-on-pink minimalism, "You Look Good" community tagline, subject lines under 25 characters, sparse copy that trusts the reader.
- **Drunk Elephant** — founder voice in welcome flows, ingredient-led education, vivid primary-color packaging shots on white.
- **Fenty Beauty** — playful-bestie tone with emoji punctuation, shade-inclusivity always in the visual, aggressive promotional cadence (6 emails/week).
- **Ouai** — Jen Atkin voice, witty subject lines, hair-specific routine slots, warm earthy palette.
- **Nécessaire** — body-care positioning, putty/sage palette, minimalist typography, ingredient-percentage callouts.
- **Aēsop** — long-form copy as a brand differentiator, no discounts ever, amber-and-cream visual identity.
- **The Ordinary** — clinical and chemical-forward, ingredient-first naming, deliberately unpretty design as the brand stance.
- **Charlotte Tilbury** — glamorous-aspirational, gold-on-pink palette, before/after used effectively, founder personality.

## Email types that matter most
- **Welcome series (3-5 emails)** — brand origin, founder note (Drunk Elephant style), ingredient education, first-product picker quiz. Beauty welcome flows do more heavy lifting than apparel because shoppers need shade and skin-type guidance.
- **Routine builders** — "morning vs night" or "your first three products" sends. High click-through.
- **Restock and shade-extension drops** — top revenue driver. Often the only email that needs to exist that week.
- **Replenishment reminders** — calculated from purchase date plus average use; "your serum is probably running low" outperforms generic restock prompts.
- **Educational / how-to** — ingredient explainers, layering guides, routine swaps for seasonal skin. Builds trust between purchases.
- **Sample-with-purchase promos** — the category's preferred lever over percent-off.

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. Vague benefit copy ("radiant, glowing, youthful skin") instead of specific actives, percentages, or textures.
2. "Self-care" framing without a real product or routine reference — beauty brands tie the moment to a step ("your 8pm cleanse").
3. Stock spa imagery (white towels, orchids, river stones) instead of macro product or real-skin swatches.
4. Subject lines that promise transformation ("Transform your skin in 7 days!") — real brands underpromise and let the reviews oversell.
5. Routine recommendations with no shade, skin-type, or concern personalization — beauty buyers expect "for combination skin" or "for medium-deep tones" in the copy.
