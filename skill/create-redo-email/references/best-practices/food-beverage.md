# Food & Beverage

## Voice & tone
Confidently weird, sensory, and irreverent. Food/bev DTC writes like the brand is a person talking to a friend at a party — not a marketer. Reading level is 6th-8th grade. Sentences are short, often fragments. Humor is the default, even for "serious" announcements.

On-brand openers:
- "We did a thing."
- "Okay, this one's a flex."
- "Cold pour. Cold weather. Cold-hearted."

Off-brand openers (AI tells):
- "We're excited to announce..."
- "Discover our delicious new flavor that combines..."
- "As a valued customer, we wanted to share..."

Forbidden phrases: "elevate your," "crafted with care," "delight your taste buds," "the perfect [anything]," "indulge in," "treat yourself to," "experience the difference."

## Visual style markers
- Color palette: Saturated and unexpected — Liquid Death's matte black + blood red, Olipop's pastel candy palette, Graza's signature green + yellow, Magic Spoon's psychedelic gradients. Avoid the safe "wellness beige."
- Typography: Bold display serifs (Graza-style chunky wedge serifs) or quirky geometric sans (Olipop). Body copy stays clean — Inter, Söhne, or default system stack. Display weights are heavy (700-900).
- Imagery: Product hero shots with personality — the can/bottle as the main character. Flat lays with food styling, never sterile e-commerce backgrounds. Lifestyle shots are aspirational but tongue-in-cheek (Liquid Death party scenes, Graza in real home kitchens). 1:1 and 4:5 dominate.
- Layout density: Medium. One product, one idea per email. Lots of negative space around hero imagery.
- Common motifs: Sticker-style badges, retro packaging callouts, recipe cards, ingredient close-ups, "before/after" pour shots.

## Layout patterns
Standard arc: bold hero image (often full-bleed product or pour shot) → punchy headline (5-8 words) → one short paragraph of brand voice → product grid OR single CTA → social proof (press logos or review) → footer with playful sign-off.

Typical section count: 3-5. Lean. Brands like Graza often hit one big image, one CTA, done.

Discount placement: Promo offers usually live in the preheader and body — rarely the subject line, because the brand voice carries the open. Welcome series exception: "20% off" appears prominently in the first email's hero.

## Copy patterns
Subject lines lean on curiosity, absurdity, or sensory cues over discounts. Examples:
> "Maybe you died?" (Liquid Death — abandoned cart)
> "WAKE UP⚡️" (Olipop)
> "Fall vibes, crisp sips 🍎🥤" (Olipop)
> "And the flavor of the month is…. 👀" (Olipop)
> "Where'd you go?" (Magic Spoon — win-back)

Preheader: Used to land the joke or add the discount the subject withheld. Examples:
> "Your cart's getting lonely. Also, 15% off."
> "New flavor. Limited cans. Read fast."
> "We made the cereal we always wanted."

Body copy length: 50-120 words is the sweet spot. Recipe and storytelling emails (Graza, Athletic Brewing) push 200-300. Never wall-of-text.

CTA verbs are direct and casual:
> "Shop the drop"
> "Grab a 12-pack"
> "Get the goods"
> "Stock up"
> "Try it"

Urgency: Light to moderate. Drops, limited flavors, and seasonal cans create natural scarcity. Avoid countdown timers and "LAST CHANCE" caps screaming — feels off-brand for this segment unless the brand identity is explicitly chaotic (Liquid Death).

## Distinguished reference brands
- **Liquid Death** — heavy metal voice applied to water. Subject lines that read like song titles.
- **Olipop** — emoji-forward, nostalgic, candy-bright. Treats every flavor launch like a product drop.
- **Magic Spoon** — cereal-box maximalism in email. Color, gradient, irreverent product copy.
- **Graza** — clean editorial design, recipe-led content, sustainability built into refill reminders.
- **Athletic Brewing** — community and lifestyle, less reliance on discounts, leans story-driven.
- **Recess** — calming pastels, mood-led copy (it's a beverage selling a feeling).
- **Poppi** — Gen Z TikTok energy, founder-led storytelling, color blocks.
- **Fishwife** — editorial illustration, tinned-fish-as-luxury reframe.

## Email types that matter most
- **Welcome series** — first impression is identity-setting. Lead with brand voice, not the discount.
- **Flavor/product drops** — treated as events with hype build-up emails the week before.
- **Recipe & usage content** — keeps consumables top-of-mind between purchases (Graza, Fishwife, Athletic Brewing).
- **Replenishment reminders** — gentle "running low?" 30-45 days post-purchase.
- **Abandoned cart** — humor-led, low pressure (Liquid Death's "Maybe you died?").
- **Press/social proof** — when the brand gets covered, that becomes an email.

## What makes this archetype's emails feel AI-generated
1. Adjective stacking ("our delicious, refreshing, perfectly-balanced sparkling beverage").
2. Sensory clichés ("explosion of flavor," "burst of refreshment," "tantalizing notes of").
3. Calling the product "innovative" or "revolutionary" — real brands let the weirdness speak for itself.
4. Hedging language ("we think you'll love," "perfect for any occasion") — real brands assert.
5. Generic seasonal copy ("Get ready for summer with...") instead of a specific cultural moment or inside joke.
